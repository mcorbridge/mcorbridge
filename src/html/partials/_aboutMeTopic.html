Software design and development was not something to which I gave much thought to as I charted my education. My passions are, and will always be: love of the outdoors, exploring, and learning. These three passions have always guided me as I worked my way through university as a camp foreman in Northern Ontario where I guided rangers down some of the wildest rivers in the country. Here I was also afforded the opportunity to experience the many forestry and wildlife programs that were run by the Ontario Ministry of Natural Resources & Forestry including wilderness survival, fighting forest fires, and census of Polar Bears on the edge of the Canadian Arctic. Throughout all these experiences I developed a deep sense and appreciation of integrity, leadership, teamwork, mentoring, perseverance and courage. To this day, no matter the circumstance, I will always look back to these early lessons and strive to embody each of these six principles in every daily challenge I encounter.
Entering my junior year Trent University in Peterborough, Ontario, I felt that pursuing a degree as a Biologist would provide me the academic credentials to continue my passion of studying the natural environment. But as I worked through courses in Zoology, Botany, Anatomy, and Biochemistry - I found these discplines seemed to leave many fundamental questions unanswered. I was fascinated in how molecules are created, and how their structure manifested the way they interacted. Thus I found myself drawn deeper into the science of Organic Chemistry. This area of science fascinated me because it relies on understanding of abstract representations of real-world behavior. It is an area that I have found I have a talent for as it combines my enhanced spatial learning style with abstract languages. Four years later I graduated with a Honours Bachelor of Science in Chemistry/Biology. I followed this degree with a Master of Science in Chemistry from York University where I specialized in chiral synthesis.
Following my Master's degree I explored new opportunities including building log cabins, working as an industrial chemist involving the creation of tons of product, building wilderness ski trails, technical specialist in capillary column chromatrography, ski instructor, and ski patrol. I was anxious to put my chemistry background to work, and I was especially interested in chemicals as pollutants. I joined the Ontario Ministry of the Environment as part of the a team responsible for measuring and monitoring air pollutants. It was during this time that I became more involved in computer programming. Environmental science is a data driven science, and a lot of data gets generated. I specialized in the measurement of trace organics using highly specialized equipment such as mass spectrometers. My primay role as a senior scientist was to design, and oversee air monitoring programs, but I could see tremendous advantages in leveraging the nascent internet technology and creating computer applications to help analyze the megabytes of data we collected.
Eventually I found myself working full time with the government creating web applications. After working on a team building the first Local Area Network for the Ontario Government,I began focusing on writing Perl scripts to create dymanic web apps. As a self taught programmer, it was challenging to transition from chemistry to code, but I was convinced that moving our widely distributed data and applications would be a tremendous accelerator in our scientific endeavours. One fateful day I chanced upon web programming language called ColdFusion. To a self-taught developer with no knowledge of SQL, relational databases, or even how to write conditional logic, ColdFusion was miraculous. I immediately started building a network of ColdFusion application servers that brought previously isolated databases and form based operations into the web. I became interested in creating ways for simple users to modify web content without the need of web developers. The term for this had yet to be discovered, but is now known as CMS. Through my activity in public forums, and the developer community in Toronto I became a beta tester for a (Spectra)CMS being developed in ColdFusion. I was offered a position on Spectra development team in Boston, Massachusetts to bring the product to market. It was a tremendous honor to work with the team that created ColdFusion, and to work with one of the fastest growing tech companies in the world, in an amazing place like Boston. After a number of full cycle release experiences with Spectra, I was moved onto the flagship product - ColdFusion - as the company prepared a major release. After the release of ColdFusion MX, I was invited to joined an elite team of engineers who were developing a for the development Flex revolutionary product for the development of rich internet applications based on the Flash Player.
Flex became widely adopted by businesses that could see the tremendous advantage of creating applications that leveraged Actionscript's Action Message Format. I was recruited out of Adobe to Sun Life Financial in Boston to lead up a team responsible for porting their in-house legacy applications to Flex. This was a tremendous opportunity for since, although I had an intimate knowledge of how Flex worked as an engineer, I didn't have real-life exposure to creating business critical applications. My learning now turned to focus on design patterns and the architecture of large multi-tiered applications. I was now transitioning into a hybrid project manager/application architect as I learned to master versioning tools like Subversion and Git, and program management tools like Jira, and agile software developent methodology, and design patterns
After almost four years at Sun Life, I had an opportunity with IBM, Boston/New York to create a team devoted to creating interactive applications. This was an exciting position that cleanly divided the team into User Experience and Creative, database, project managers, middle-tier java developers, and finally my team - front end specialists. The mandate was to focus on creating desktop Adobe AIR applications that could be easily distributed to our clients and which would encapsulate powerful on- and off-line data synchronization. In addition, I became a strong advocate of Inversion of Control / Dependency Injection frameworks. For four years I worked with teams, and led teams that created mission critical applications for fortune 500 companies around the world.
Leaving IBM, I changed direction and concentrated on mastering mobile (Android), and the HTML5 technologies. The advent of the iPad, and the finalization of the HTML standard set the stage for a rapid decline in AIR and Flash projects. I took on a contract position with Under Armour where I could work with a team developing their primary web site which was responsible for $700k in transactions / day. The UA site was an enormous, and complex project that combined state-of-the-art HTML5 technology with 10 yr old legacy code. The amount of technical debt made this a very challenging experience and one where I could leverage my past experience in ColdFusion and Flex, as I learned and experimented with the newest javascript frameworks such as Backbone.js, Emberjs.
It was while I was at UA that I decided that I wanted to concentrate on two technologies: Android and AngularJS. I left to focus on developing applications that would provide a seamless integration between web and mobile applications, using Google's cloud platform. With a private investor, I have created an application that shares the AES encrypted information between a Android java, and an AngularJS/javascript implementation. I have also developed web/mobile prototypes for Equinox, SoundShapes and NextHabitat.
